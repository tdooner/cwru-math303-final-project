Mersenne, And His Numbers

  • Marin Mersenne (1588-1648) was a prolific French mathematician and music theorist.
  • Lived in the same era as Galileo, Huygens, Fermat, and religious science oppression.
  • Mersenne Numbers are of the form $$M_n = 2^{n} - 1$$
  • The Mersenne Numbers that are prime are called Mersenne Primes.

Mersenne Primes

  • When is \( M_n = 2^n - 1\) prime?
  • If \( n \) is composite, it can be written as \( a \times b \)
  • \( 2^{ab}-1\) factors to \((2^{a}-1)(2^a+2^{2a}+\ldots+2^{(b-1)a}+1)\)
  • So if \( n \) is composite, so too is \( M_n \).
    • The converse is not true.
    • When searching for Mersenne Primes, only bother with prime \( n \).

Mersenne's Conjecture

  • Of the fifty-five primes \(0 < n \leq 257\), Mersenne conjectured that \(M_p\) is prime for \(p=2, 3, 5, 7, 13, 17, 19, 31, 67, 127, 257\)
  • How Mersenne picked these numbers is a bit mysterious.
  • "\( M_p \) is prime if and only if \( p \) is a prime of one of the forms \( 2^k \pm 1 \) or \( 2^{2k} \pm 3 \)"?
  • Except, he omitted \( p = 61 \)...
  • So, given the lack of computational aide Mersenne had, is his list correct?

Mersenne's List Isn't Correct

  • No, but it's pretty close. Mersenne conjectured that \(M_p\) is prime for \(p=2, 3, 5, 7, 13, 17, 19, 31, 67, 127, 257\)
  • Mersenne: 235713171931 67 127257
    Corrected:23571317193161 89107127
  • New Mersenne Conjecture attempts to be more comprehensive. It says that if two of these are true, the third is as well:
    • \( p = 2^k\pm1 \) or \( p = 2^{2k}\pm3 \).
    • \( (2^{p}+1)/3 \) is prime.
    • \( M_p \) is prime.
    • Tested for all \( p < 100000 \), \( k < 4000 \).

Properties of Mersenne Primes

  • Lenstra-Pomerance-Wagstaff Conjecture: There are infinitely many Mersenne Primes.
    • Number of primes approximated by \( e^{\gamma}\cdot\log\log(x)/\log(2) \) (\( \gamma = 0.57721... \))
  • Perfect numbers have a 1-to-1 correspondence with even perfect numbers.
    • Given \( M_p \), \( 2^{p-1}(2^p-1) \) is a perfect number.
    • p = 2, (2^1)(2^2-1) = 6
    • p = 3, (2^2)(2^3-1) = 28
    • p = 5, (2^5)(2^5-1) = 496
    • p = 7, (2^6)(2^7-1) = 8128
